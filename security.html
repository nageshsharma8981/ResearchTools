Security, in plain language

The question that matters most: can another website, another open tab, or we ourselves ever get your AI provider key? Short answer — no. Here is exactly how that works, and where the honest limits are.

Where your key lives, and where it goes

When you paste an AI provider key into API settings, it is saved only in your own browser's local storage on this one device. When you run an AI tool, your browser sends the request — with that key — directly to the provider you chose (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, etc.). It goes nowhere else.

Your browser — key + your text, over HTTPS → Your AI provider

Your key is never sent to ItsMyResearch's servers. We have no code that receives it, so there is nothing on our side to store, log, leak, or have stolen. You can verify this yourself: open your browser's developer tools → Network tab, run a tool, and you'll see the request go straight to the provider's domain — never to ours.

Can another website or open tab read my key?

No — the browser physically prevents it. Browsers enforce the same-origin policy: local storage is walled off per website. Data saved by itsmyresearch.com can only be read by itsmyresearch.com. A shopping site, a social network, or any malicious page open in another tab cannot reach into our storage — the browser blocks it at the engine level, no matter what that page tries. This is the same protection that stops other sites from reading your bank's session.

We also add belt-and-braces on our side so nothing can bridge that wall:

  • No cross-site messaging. We register no channels (no postMessage listeners, no third-party frames) that another page could use to talk to our storage.
  • A strict Content-Security-Policy. The browser is told to block plug-in objects, disallow our pages from being embedded in a frame (frame-ancestors 'none', plus X-Frame-Options: DENY), and restrict where scripts may load from — closing the usual routes an attacker uses to run code on a page.
  • Escape-everything rendering. The one way a site's own storage can be stolen is a script-injection (XSS) flaw on that site. Everything we display — including AI output and anything you paste — is HTML-escaped before it is shown, so text can never turn into running code.
  • HTTPS everywhere (with HSTS), so the key is encrypted in transit and can't be read off the network.

What we honestly can't protect against

No website can defend against threats that live on your own device or in your own browser. Being straight about this matters more than sounding bulletproof:

  • Malicious browser extensions. An extension you install can, by design, read the contents of pages and storage you visit — any site, not just ours. Only install extensions you trust, and review their permissions.
  • Malware or another user on your computer. Software running on your machine, or someone with access to your unlocked device, can read browser storage. Keep your device secure and locked.
  • Shared or public computers. Your key persists in that browser until cleared. On a shared machine, remove it when you're done (API settings → clear the key, or your browser's "clear site data").

The zero-key option. If any of the above worries you, use the free built-in browser model or a local model (Ollama / LM Studio). Then there is no key at all, and your research text is processed entirely on your own device — nothing, not even to your AI provider, ever leaves your machine.

Accounts and payments

  • Passwords are stored only as a salted scrypt hash — we cannot read them. Sign-in tokens are stored hashed, in httpOnly cookies a page's JavaScript can't read.
  • Payments go through Razorpay. Card and UPI details go straight to them; we never see or store them — only a payment id and your plan status.
  • Your research content — documents, transcripts, drafts — is processed in your browser and never uploaded to us. The only thing that reaches your AI provider is what you submit to a tool, under your own account with them.
